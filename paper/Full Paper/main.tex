\begin{abstract}
Quantum machine learning (QML) is frequently proposed for expensive physical
simulation and trajectory-design problems, but a feature map or a statevector
result alone does not demonstrate practical value. This paper reports a closed,
governed public-data benchmark of quantum-kernel methods for human-rated
cislunar trajectory-correction planning. The study couples a source-bound
simulator, grouped development splits, fold-local preprocessing, physics-derived
and classical controls, preregistered retention rules, and explicit evidence
boundaries. The simulator credibility chain accepted 67 eligible public-data
checks with zero failures. In the preregistered Gate 5 benchmark, the Q01
fidelity-style quantum kernel attained mean normalized root-mean-square error
(NRMSE) 0.646614, compared with 0.008739 for the C06 physics-residual control;
no regime qualified for advance. A projected quantum-kernel successor (Q01b)
attained 0.661207 versus 0.006833 for C06, and a feasibility-only quantum kernel
had recall 0.108860 at the frozen threshold, so neither exploratory track
advanced. Sixteen subsequent, prospectively bounded development-only candidates
also completed as valid negatives. The strongest apparent residual gain,
ORFRK-08-R2, did not clear its matched classical-control requirement. The
contribution is therefore a reproducible negative benchmark and a claim
discipline: apparent QML gains in high-consequence trajectory applications must
survive physics-based controls, matched dequantizations, grouped leakage
protection, and frozen eligibility rules. The study does not claim quantum
advantage, propellant savings, mission-loop validity, flight readiness, hardware
performance, or a new QML invention.
\keywords{Quantum machine learning \and quantum kernels \and cislunar trajectory design \and reproducible benchmarking \and negative results}
\end{abstract}
