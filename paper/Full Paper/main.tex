\begin{abstract}
This study tests whether quantum machine learning (QML) kernels improve a
public-data benchmark for crewed Earth--Moon trajectory correction. The target
is robust total correction delta-v: modeled velocity change after accounting
for uncertainty. Related mission cases shared a data fold; preprocessing used
only training rows; thresholds were fixed before outcomes; and calibration and
final-test rows remained locked. The simulator chain passed 67 eligible checks;
one event lacked eligible evidence and was classified neither pass nor failure. On development
data, the primary fidelity kernel (Q01) produced mean normalized
root-mean-square error (NRMSE) 0.646614, while the physics-residual classical
control (C06) produced 0.008739. NRMSE divides root-mean-square error by the
target standard deviation; lower is better. The projected-kernel
follow-up (Q01b) produced 0.661207 versus 0.006833 for C06. The feasibility-only
quantum kernel (FQK) retained 10.9\% of actually feasible cases at the fixed
0.5 threshold. Sixteen bounded successors failed predeclared
advancement criteria; the strongest apparent gain did not pass its matched
classical-control threshold. This does not show that QML is universally
ineffective; it shows that the tested representations did not justify quantum
complexity for this frozen task. The societal contribution is a transparent
safeguard against unsupported AI claims in high-consequence decisions:
preserving provenance, reporting negative outcomes, and separating crew
requirements and human authorization from model scores. This study makes no claim of
quantum advantage, propellant savings, flight readiness, hardware performance,
or a new QML invention.
\keywords{Quantum machine learning \and human-centered AI \and decision support \and cislunar trajectory design \and reproducible benchmarking \and negative results}
\end{abstract}
